Logline: In 1893 Chicago, a determined socialite, a pioneering architect, and a collective of visionary women defy societal expectations to build a groundbreaking exhibition at the World's Fair, proving women's ingenuity and power to a skeptical world.

Three-Act Plot Outline:

Act I: The Challenge and the Vision
*   **Introduction of the main characters:** Bertha Palmer, a formidable socialite and leader; Sophia Hayden, a brilliant but overlooked architect; and the diverse women of the Board of Lady Managers.
*   **The setting:** Chicago, 1890, as preparations for the World's Columbian Exposition begin. The male-dominated organizing committee reluctantly agrees to a "Woman's Department" but offers minimal resources and a dismissive attitude.
*   **The spark:** Bertha Palmer, appointed president of the Board of Lady Managers, sees this as an opportunity to showcase women's achievements on a global stage, not just a token gesture. She envisions a dedicated "Woman's Building."
*   **Initial obstacles:** Resistance from the male organizers, skepticism from the public, and internal disagreements within the Board about the scope and ambition of their project. Sophia Hayden, a young, unproven architect, faces prejudice and doubt when she submits her design for the Woman's Building.
*   **Rising action:** Bertha champions Sophia's design, fighting for its approval against established male architects. The Board secures a meager budget and a challenging timeline. They begin the arduous task of gathering exhibits from women worldwide, facing logistical nightmares and cultural barriers.

Act II: Building the Dream
*   **Construction and conflict:** The Woman's Building begins construction. Sophia faces immense pressure, tight deadlines, and condescending male contractors who doubt her abilities. Bertha navigates political maneuvering and fundraising challenges, constantly battling for more resources and recognition for the women's efforts.
*   **Internal and external pressures:** The Board faces criticism from traditionalists who believe women should not be involved in such public endeavors. Some members of the Board struggle with the radical nature of their undertaking, leading to internal tensions. Bertha and Sophia form a strong, albeit sometimes strained, partnership, pushing each other to overcome obstacles.
*   **Moments of triumph and despair:** Small victories, like securing a crucial exhibit or overcoming a construction setback, are interspersed with moments of doubt and near-failure. Sophia battles exhaustion and self-doubt, while Bertha faces public scrutiny and personal sacrifices.
*   **The World's Congress of Representative Women:** The Board organizes this groundbreaking congress, bringing together women from around the world to discuss pressing issues, further solidifying their impact and challenging global perceptions of women's roles.

Act III: Legacy and Triumph
*   **Opening Day:** The World's Columbian Exposition opens. The Woman's Building stands as a testament to their perseverance, a beautiful and inspiring structure filled with groundbreaking exhibits. The initial reception is mixed, with some still dismissive, but many are genuinely impressed.
*   **Growing recognition:** As the fair progresses, the Woman's Building becomes a popular and influential attraction. Visitors are moved by the displays of women's art, industry, and intellectual achievements. Sophia Hayden gains recognition for her architectural genius, and Bertha Palmer is hailed as a visionary leader.
*   **The fight for lasting impact:** Despite the success, the women realize the fight for equality is far from over. They use the momentum of the fair to advocate for women's rights and opportunities beyond the Exposition.
*   **Resolution:** The fair concludes, and while the Woman's Building is eventually demolished, its impact is undeniable. The film ends with a sense of hope and the understanding that the efforts of these women laid crucial groundwork for future generations. Bertha, Sophia, and the Board members reflect on their journey, knowing they have irrevocably changed the conversation about women's capabilities.
